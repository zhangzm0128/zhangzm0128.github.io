Abstract

Protein structure prediction (PSP) is predicting the three-dimensional of protein from its amino acid sequence only based on the information hidden in the protein sequence. One of the efficient tools to describe this information is protein energy functions. Despite the advancements in biology and computer science, PSP is still a challenging problem due to its large protein conformation space and inaccurate energy functions. In this study, PSP is treated as a many-objective optimization problem and four conflicting energy functions are used as different objectives to be optimized. A novel Pareto-dominance-archive and Coordinated-selection-strategy-based Many-objective-optimizer (PCM) is proposed to perform the conformation search. In it, convergence and diversity-based selection metrics are used to enable PCM to find near-native proteins with well-distributed energy values, while a Pareto-dominance-based archive is proposed to save more potential conformations that can guide the search to more promising conformation areas. The experimental results on thirty-four benchmark proteins demonstrate the significant superiority of PCM in comparison with other single, multiple, and many-objective evolutionary algorithms. Additionally, the inherent characteristics of iterative search of PCM can also give more insights into the dynamic progress of protein folding besides the final predicted static tertiary structure. All these confirm that PCM is a fast, easy-to-use, and fruitful solution generation method for PSP.

Protein Structure Prediction

Protein structure prediction (PSP) is predicting the three-dimensional of protein from its amino acid sequence only based on the information hidden in the protein sequence.

Template-based modeling (TBM) and free-modeling (FM) are two major computational methods used in PSP. The former relies on the identification of homologous proteins in Protein Data Bank to a great extent. The latter, also termed as ab-initio prediction, can cope with such circumstances productively, as it predicts the tertiary structure of a protein from its amino acid sequence directly.

PCM Overview


The figure shows the framework of PCM for PSP. Firstly, initial proteins are generated from the amino-acid-sequence by limiting proteins, torsion angles using a secondary structure prediction method, namely PSIPRED, to reduce the search space. The backbone angle restriction for $\phi$ and $\psi$ obtained by PSIPRED is summarized in the Supplementary File.

Then, the population of proteins is used to generate new individuals (i.e., solutions) through a mating selection, mutation, and crossover. The mutation and crossover operators used in PCM are polynomial mutation and simulated binary crossover, respectively. Thereafter, an environmental selection strategy chooses the optimal population from all individuals to be survived into the next generation. In addition, a Pareto-based archive is used to save more potential conformations in our method. The environmental selection strategy is used to update the archive from the new generation based on Pareto dominance and a crowding distance mechanism.

Finally, a clustering method based on the comparison of the protein distance matrix is used to select the most representative conformation from the archive, because the conformation of the native protein is an optimal point rather than a Pareto front.

Experimental Results

The comparison of the best predicted conformation (green) and the clustered one (pink) with the native conformation (red) for four typical proteins with different structures of a varying number of $\alpha$-helices and $\beta$-sheets. It is noteworthy that the $\alpha$-helix portions of the predicted results, both in the best and clustered one, are nearly identical to the native conformation. This strongly indicates the superior ability of PCM in predicting the $\alpha$-helix. Conformation comparison figures for all thirty-four proteins are provided in the Supplementary File.

It should be pointed out that the real Pareto front of PSP is unknown due to the inaccurate energy functions. To find out the real native conformation, the archive in PCM saves as many potential conformations as possible, which are widely distributed on the obtained Pareto front. PCM can obtain fruitful conformations from multiple energy functions to achieve the near-native conformation.

PCM can provide the dynamic folding process of proteins that cannot be achieved by other methods. Thus, such inherent characteristics of iterative search of PCM can give some insights into the dynamic progress of protein folding for specialists in biology and life science.
